A PENNY For My Thoughts

Chapter 1 — Free Preview
April 2013 — Washington State Armed robbery comes with a rush that's hard to explain to anyone who hasn't lived it, a violent surge that sharpens every sense at once until your heartbeat pounds in your throat and your thoughts narrow down to one thing—control. You either have it, or you don't. Mine was steady. Curtis' wasn't. I could feel the difference between us even before anything went wrong, the tension sitting just beneath his surface like something waiting for an excuse. Sweat soaked through my clothes, trapped beneath the Kevlar vest under my Nike sweats, while the ski mask pressed tight against my face and made every breath feel heavier than it should have. Still, I kept my Glock steady, scanning the room, reading movement, watching hands, tracking fear the way I'd learned to trust. Five employees lay stretched across the grimy carpet—three women, two men, all white, all silent, all completely under our control. Just minutes ago, they had been moving like it was any other morning. Now they weren't moving at all. Curtis worked behind me, quick and efficient as he cleared the safe, stuffing stacks of cash into a duffle bag with practiced speed. He was good at that part—fast hands, no hesitation—but there was always something just beneath it, something that didn't belong to the job itself. Something waiting. I kept the room covered, sweeping angles, checking corners, staying ahead of anything that might shift. "It's been four minutes. Let's move." "Yeah, yeah… I'm done," Curtis muttered, though there was something in his tone that didn't match the words, like he wasn't quite ready for it to end. That was Curtis. He didn't just do the job. He fed off it. I stepped toward the exit, positioning myself to clear the door with my grip firm on the Glock, while Curtis followed close behind with the duffle bag slung over his shoulder. For a brief second, everything felt exactly how it was supposed to feel—tight, controlled, finished. Then it broke. One of the male employees lunged forward and grabbed Curtis by the ankle with desperate strength, and just like that, the balance shifted. Curtis went down hard, crashing into me as we slammed against the wall beside the door. The man moved faster than I expected—faster than fear usually allows—as he pulled a snub-nosed .38 from an ankle holster and rolled beneath a desk, trying to create space. I didn't hesitate. I fired twice, both rounds landing center mass before he could even bring the weapon up, his body collapsing as the gun slipped from his hand. But I didn't trust "down." I stepped forward and put one more round through his forehead, making sure it was finished. Behind me, Curtis snapped—but it wasn't panic or fear. It was something else…

Unplugged: 30 Days of Chaos

Chapter 1 — Free Preview
Reggie Hayes woke up with that same fucked-up feeling he always got when his alarm hadn't gone off but his body knew he was already late. Sunlight blasted through the cheap plastic blinds he'd been meaning to replace for three years now. Dust drifted lazily through the beam like slow snow. Forty-seven years old and still waking up in an apartment that looked like temporary living. No woman beside him. No kids down the hall. Just the low hum of Fish's tank filter and Arnold's fat orange body sprawled across the foot of the bed like he owned the place. "Move, you heavyweight," Reggie muttered. The cat opened one lazy eye, unimpressed. Twenty pounds of fur and attitude. Arnold stayed because Reggie kept the bowl full. That was the entire relationship. Reggie shoved the blankets aside and reached for his phone. 7:43. "Shit." He was supposed to be at the warehouse by eight. Third time late this month meant Eddie's disappointed face. That quiet look that said I stuck my neck out for you, Reg. Reggie jumped up and rushed through his morning routine on autopilot. Shower. Polo. Khakis. Keys. Coffee. Coffee always came first. He dumped two heavy scoops of sugar into the mug. No cream. Diabetes had already taken enough people in his family. The first sip burned his tongue but woke up his brain. While drinking, he flipped open his laptop—habit more than necessity. Reggie always checked traffic before heading out. He clicked the browser. Nothing happened. "No internet?" he muttered. He checked the router. Lights were on. Weird. He grabbed his phone. Instagram wouldn't load. Twitter wouldn't load. His banking app returned an error. The text message he sent to Mike sat there undelivered. Reggie frowned. "That ain't right." Cell service didn't just disappear. A faint unease crept into his chest. He grabbed the remote and turned on the TV. A news broadcast flickered onto the screen. The anchor looked tense, her professional smile gone. "—reports are coming in from across the country that major digital networks have gone offline—" The screen flickered. She glanced off camera. "—we are being told this disruption is affecting communications infrastructure nationwide—" Static swallowed the broadcast. The image returned for a split second. Then disappeared again. Now the screen showed a government seal. A voice crackled through the speaker. "This is a federal emergency broadcast. All citizens are advised to remain inside their homes until further instruction." The signal cut out. Dead air. Reggie stared at the screen. "The hell?" He flipped channels. Same message. Or nothing. Just static. No internet. No cell signal. Now emergency broadcasts. A cold knot formed in his stomach…
